A marketplace that lets Nepal's farmers sell directly to restaurants, hotels, and schools — with payment guaranteed by code before a single vegetable is loaded onto a truck.

01 — The Problem

Farmers grow the food.
Middlemen take the money.

Right now, a tomato that leaves a farm in Dhading at NPR 15–25 per kg arrives at a Kathmandu restaurant for NPR 99–149 per kg. The farmer receives roughly one-fifth of what the buyer pays. Not because of transport costs — those are real but small — but because five to seven layers of people in the middle each take a cut.

The farmer has no way to know what the market price actually is today. They have no direct connection to buyers in the city. And even when they do sell — to the government dairy, for example — there is no way to make that buyer actually pay on time. The state-owned Dairy Development Corporation (DDC) has owed farmers billions of rupees in unpaid dues for years, peaking at NPR 3 billion combined with private dairies in recent years, with farmers going unpaid for up to nine months at a time.

Three things cause this: Farmers don't know the market price, so they can't negotiate. They have no way to reach buyers directly, so they're forced to accept whatever the local collector offers. And payment promises — especially from government bodies — are unenforceable, so buyers can delay or simply not pay for months.
02 — What Khet Does

Three problems.
One solution.

Khet is an app where farmers list their crops for sale. Buyers — restaurants, hotels, schools, anyone — browse and place orders. The buyer's money is held securely by code the moment they click "order." The farmer ships knowing the money is already locked and waiting. When the buyer confirms delivery, the money releases instantly — in under a second.

No waiting. No promises. No middlemen. The code handles the trust that used to require either personal relationships or expensive lawyers.

03 — Key Concepts

What the tech
actually means

Every unfamiliar word below, explained without assuming you know anything about crypto or blockchain.

Concept 01

Blockchain — a shared record no one can erase

Imagine a notebook that thousands of computers around the world all hold an identical copy of at the same time. When something new is written in it, every copy updates at once. Nobody can secretly change or delete a line because everyone else's copy would instantly disagree with the change. That's a blockchain — a shared record that nobody controls alone.

For Khet, this means: once a deal is recorded, nobody — not Khet the company, not the government, not a hacker — can alter the terms or erase the payment obligation.

Think of a classroom attendance register that every student, every teacher, and every parent holds a copy of. You can't secretly cross out your absence because everyone else's copy still shows it.
Concept 02

Solana — the specific blockchain Khet runs on

There are many blockchains. The oldest, Bitcoin, takes 10 minutes to confirm a transaction and costs the equivalent of NPR 600–700 per transaction in fees. That's completely unusable for a farmer selling NPR 500 worth of vegetables.

Solana is a different blockchain — much newer and much faster. A transaction settles in about 0.4 seconds and costs roughly NPR 0.02 in fees. Even a very small sale is economically viable. That's why Khet uses it.

Like comparing a bullock cart (Bitcoin — slow, expensive) to a bullet train (Solana — fast, cheap) for sending a letter across the country.
Concept 03

Stablecoin (USDC) — digital money that doesn't fluctuate in value

Most people have heard that Bitcoin goes up 30% one week and crashes 40% the next. That would be terrible for a farmer who needs to pay for seeds next Tuesday. A stablecoin is completely different: 1 USDC always equals exactly 1 US Dollar, no matter what. It's just regular money stored on a blockchain instead of in a bank.

Khet uses USDC so farmers are paid in stable value. They can convert it to Nepali rupees through exchange partners — the same way people transfer money through eSewa or Khalti today, just different rails.

Like eSewa balance. 1 eSewa rupee = 1 real rupee, always. You don't worry about your eSewa balance suddenly losing value. USDC works the same way, just on a blockchain instead of eSewa's servers.
Concept 04

Escrow — a lockbox that holds money until conditions are met

When you buy a house, you don't hand the full amount directly to the seller on day one, hoping they'll hand you the keys later. A neutral party — traditionally a lawyer — holds the money. Once the keys are handed over and everything is confirmed, the lawyer releases the money to the seller. That holding arrangement is called escrow.

In Khet, there's no lawyer. Instead, code running on Solana plays that role. When a buyer places an order, their payment moves into a digital lockbox controlled entirely by that code. The buyer can't take the money back. Khet the company can't touch it either. Only the condition — "buyer confirms delivery" — unlocks it.

Like buying something on OLX. You wouldn't wire the money to a stranger before seeing the item. Khet's escrow holds the money in code until both sides confirm the deal happened.
Concept 05

Smart Contract — a contract that enforces itself automatically

A contract is normally a document that says "if X happens, then Y must happen." But enforcing it requires lawyers, courts, and months of time. A smart contract is the same idea — "if X happens, then Y happens" — except the logic is written in computer code that runs automatically on the blockchain. There's no way to ignore it, override it, or delay it. The code runs when its conditions are met. Full stop.

For Khet, the smart contract says: "When the buyer taps Confirm Delivery, release the locked payment to the farmer's wallet." That rule cannot be bent by anyone.

Like a vending machine. You put in NPR 50 and press B3. The machine gives you chips automatically. It doesn't ask for a manager's approval. The mechanism itself enforces the deal.
Concept 06

Wallet — your account and identity on the blockchain

A crypto wallet is like a bank account and a username combined into one thing. It has two parts: a public address (like an account number — you share this so people can send you money) and a private key (like your PIN or password — you never share this, ever). On Khet, farmers get a wallet where payments arrive the moment a delivery is confirmed.

Farmers who aren't comfortable with crypto get a wallet created for them automatically in the background — they just see a phone number login, like any other app. The blockchain part is invisible.

Your eSewa mobile number is your "public address" — anyone can send money to it. Your eSewa PIN is your "private key" — only you can spend from it. Same concept.
Concept 07

Kalimati Oracle — live price data from Nepal's biggest vegetable market

The Kalimati Fruits and Vegetable Market publishes official wholesale prices every morning for every major crop — straight from the Government of Nepal's Ministry of Agriculture. These are public numbers.

Khet's oracle is a simple background service that fetches those numbers at 6am every day and shows them in the app. The word "oracle" just means "a system that brings outside real-world information into the blockchain." When a farmer in Dhading opens Khet, they see that Kalimati's tomato price today is NPR 65–75 per kg — so they know not to accept NPR 20 from the local collector.

Like checking Hamrobazaar or Sasto Deal before selling your old laptop. You know the going market price, so nobody can lowball you.
Concept 08

PDA (Program Derived Address) — the vault nobody has the key to

When a buyer locks money in escrow on Solana, it goes into a special type of account called a PDA. What makes it special: nobody has the private key to it. Not the buyer, not the farmer, not Khet the company. The only thing that can move money out of it is the smart contract code itself — and only when the coded conditions are met.

This is what makes Khet's escrow genuinely trustless. The money isn't held by Khet in a bank account that Khet could theoretically access. It's held by math and code.

Like a bank vault that physically cannot open until two conditions are both true: the farmer confirms shipment, and the buyer confirms receipt. Nobody holds the key — the door just opens when both conditions are satisfied.
04 — How It Works

A deal from farm
to payment, step by step

01

Farmer opens the app and sees today's real price

Ram, a farmer in Dhading, opens Khet. The app pulls the Kalimati oracle data from this morning: tomatoes are at NPR 65–75 per kg at wholesale. A local collector came to his farm offering NPR 20. Ram now knows the actual market rate.

02

Farmer creates a listing on the blockchain

Ram lists: 500 kg of tomatoes, NPR 45 per kg, Dhading district, available Thursday. He taps Submit. This listing is written to the Solana blockchain — permanently and publicly. Every buyer in Nepal can now see it.

On-chain: list_produce()
03

A buyer in Kathmandu finds the listing and places an order

Sunrise Restaurant in Thamel needs 200 kg of tomatoes. They find Ram's listing — NPR 45 vs Kalimati's NPR 65. They tap Order. Their wallet shows a confirmation screen: NPR 9,000 + 1% platform fee.

04

Payment locks into the escrow vault — automatically

The moment the buyer confirms, their USDC moves from their wallet into a PDA vault controlled only by the smart contract. The buyer cannot get it back. Khet cannot touch it. Ram gets a notification: "Order confirmed. Your payment is secured in escrow."

On-chain: place_order() → PDA vault locked
05

Ram loads his tomatoes and delivers

This part is entirely physical — it happens in the real world. Ram loads his truck and drives to Thamel. The blockchain just waits, holding the money safely.

06

Buyer confirms delivery received

The restaurant manager inspects the tomatoes. Satisfied, they tap Confirm Delivery in the app. This sends a transaction to Solana that triggers the smart contract's release condition.

On-chain: confirm_delivery()
07

Ram receives the money in 0.4 seconds

The smart contract automatically releases NPR 8,910 (minus 1% platform fee) to Ram's wallet. Instantly. No bank transfer waiting period. No broken promise. No "we'll pay you next month." The transaction is recorded permanently on the Solana blockchain — visible to anyone, forever.

PDA vault → farmer's wallet · automatic · irreversible
What if something goes wrong? If the buyer never taps Confirm Delivery within 7 days, the smart contract automatically refunds them — no human decision needed. If there's a real dispute (wrong quantity, quality issue), either party can raise it. Funds stay frozen while a Khet team member reviews the evidence and makes a decision. The rules are baked into the code before any deal starts.

07 — Honest Challenges

What could
actually go wrong

The technical side is proven — escrow systems secure billions of dollars globally in decentralised finance every day. The real challenges are product and adoption challenges:

Smartphone access. Many farmers in remote areas don't own smartphones. Solution: cooperative officers and agri-extension workers act as intermediaries, using the app on behalf of farmers in their area.

Crypto wallets are confusing. Most farmers have never used a blockchain wallet and shouldn't have to learn one. Solution: hide the blockchain completely behind a standard phone-number login. The wallet is created automatically in the background.

Converting USDC to NPR. Farmers need real rupees, not stablecoins. Solution: partner with a licensed fiat on-ramp — similar to how remittance services work today. USDC arrives in the wallet, they tap "Withdraw to eSewa/bank account," done.

Regulatory. Nepal's crypto regulations are evolving. We build on the assumption of compliance and partner with a licensed Nepali financial institution from day one.

These are normal startup execution challenges with clear paths. They're not blockers.
